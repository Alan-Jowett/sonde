        Threat-Model-First Onboarding of Constrained Devices
      over Encrypted but Unauthenticated Local Transports

Abstract

   Constrained devices are frequently onboarded using local transports
   that provide confidentiality but limited or no peer authentication,
   such as Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) "Just Works" pairing or
   proprietary short-range radios. While these transports are often
   described as "secure", they do not provide device identity
   guarantees, and naive onboarding designs frequently conflate
   encrypted channels with authenticated peers.

   This document describes observed failure modes, threat models, and
   design patterns for bootstrapping trust in constrained devices over
   encrypted but unauthenticated local transports. It does not define a
   new protocol. Instead, it documents practical lessons from deployed
   systems, including delegation of onboarding authority, handling of
   unavoidable race conditions, bootstrap message semantics, and
   transition to steady-state secure communication. The goal is to
   provide guidance to designers of constrained device onboarding
   systems and to clarify the security properties such systems can and
   cannot achieve.

1.  Introduction

   Constrained devices such as sensors, actuators, and embedded
   controllers are commonly onboarded into a larger system using
   short-range local transports. These transports are attractive
   because they are widely available, energy-efficient, and often
   supported by commodity user devices such as smartphones.

   In practice, many such transports provide confidentiality and
   integrity for messages exchanged between two endpoints, but do not
   provide strong guarantees about the identity of the peer during
   initial contact. Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) pairing using the "Just
   Works" association model is a common example: the resulting link is
   encrypted, but is vulnerable to active man-in-the-middle attacks
   during pairing and does not provide meaningful peer authentication.

   A recurring design error in onboarding systems is the assumption
   that an encrypted local channel implies that the communicating peer
   is the intended device. This assumption leads to protocols that
   implicitly rely on properties the transport does not provide,
   resulting in race conditions, identity confusion, or brittle
   security checks layered on top of the channel.

   At the same time, many real-world systems intentionally accept
   these limitations in exchange for usability, cost, or power-
   consumption constraints. Rather than attempting to eliminate all
   attacks -- which is often infeasible -- such systems instead aim to
   bound the impact of attacks, ensure recoverability, and concentrate
   trust decisions in components capable of enforcing policy.

   This document captures design patterns and failure modes observed
   in deployed constrained-device onboarding systems that operate over
   encrypted but unauthenticated local transports. It focuses on
   threat-model-first reasoning, explicit separation of bootstrap and
   steady-state security properties, and the use of delegated
   authorities during onboarding. The document does not propose new
   cryptographic mechanisms or replace existing standards such as
   EDHOC, OSCORE, or Bluetooth Security Manager Protocol (SMP);
   instead, it complements them by clarifying the assumptions required
   for safe composition.
