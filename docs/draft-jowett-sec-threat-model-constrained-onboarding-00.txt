      Security Patterns for Constrained Device Onboarding
      over Unauthenticated Local Transports

Abstract

   Constrained devices are frequently onboarded using local
   transports that provide confidentiality but limited or no peer
   authentication, such as Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) "Just Works"
   pairing or proprietary short-range radios.  While these
   transports are often described as "secure", they do not provide
   device identity guarantees, and naive onboarding designs
   frequently conflate encrypted channels with authenticated peers.

   This document describes common failure modes, threat models, and
   design patterns for bootstrapping trust in constrained devices
   over encrypted but unauthenticated local transports.  It does
   not define a new protocol.  Instead, it documents design
   patterns identified through analysis of constrained-device
   onboarding requirements, including delegation of onboarding
   authority, handling of unavoidable race conditions, bootstrap
   message semantics, and transition to steady-state secure
   communication.  The goal is to provide guidance to designers of
   constrained device onboarding systems and to clarify the
   security properties such systems can and cannot achieve.

1.  Introduction

   Constrained devices such as sensors, actuators, and embedded
   controllers are commonly onboarded into a larger system using
   short-range local transports.  These transports are attractive
   because they are widely available, energy-efficient, and often
   supported by commodity user devices such as smartphones.

   In practice, many such transports provide confidentiality and
   integrity for messages exchanged between two endpoints, but do
   not provide strong guarantees about the identity of the peer
   during initial contact.  Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) pairing
   using the "Just Works" association model is a common example:
   the resulting link is encrypted, but is vulnerable to active
   man-in-the-middle attacks during pairing and does not provide
   meaningful peer authentication.

   A recurring design error in onboarding systems is the assumption
   that an encrypted local channel implies that the communicating
   peer is the intended device.  This assumption leads to protocols
   that implicitly rely on properties the transport does not
   provide, resulting in race conditions, identity confusion, or
   brittle security checks layered on top of the channel.

   At the same time, many real-world systems intentionally accept
   these limitations in exchange for usability, cost, or power-
   consumption constraints.  Rather than attempting to eliminate
   all attacks -- which is often infeasible -- such systems instead
   aim to bound the impact of attacks, ensure recoverability, and
   concentrate trust decisions in components capable of enforcing
   policy.

   This document captures design patterns and common failure modes
   for constrained-device onboarding systems that operate over
   encrypted but unauthenticated local transports.  It focuses on
   threat-model-first reasoning, explicit separation of bootstrap
   and steady-state security properties, and the use of delegated
   authorities during onboarding.  The document does not propose
   new cryptographic mechanisms or replace existing standards such
   as EDHOC [RFC9528], OSCORE [RFC8613], or Bluetooth Security
   Manager Protocol (SMP) [BT-CORE]; instead, it complements them
   by clarifying the assumptions required for safe composition.

   The key words "MUST", "MUST NOT", "REQUIRED", "SHALL", "SHALL
   NOT", "SHOULD", "SHOULD NOT", "RECOMMENDED", "NOT RECOMMENDED",
   "MAY", and "OPTIONAL" in this document are to be interpreted as
   described in BCP 14 [RFC2119] [RFC8174] when, and only when,
   they appear in all capitals, as shown here.


2.  Terminology

   This document uses the following terms:

   Authorization Authority:  The entity that serves as the root of
      trust for a deployment.  It stores device credentials,
      validates enrollment requests, and enforces policy.
      Examples include a local gateway, a network controller, or
      a cloud service.

   Constrained Device:  A device with limited processing, memory,
      power, or connectivity resources [RFC7228] that is the
      subject of onboarding.  Before onboarding, the device has
      no pre-existing identity or credentials.

   Mobile Agent:  A user-operated device (typically a smartphone)
      that mediates onboarding on behalf of the authorization
      authority.  The mobile agent holds a revocable credential
      issued by the authority.

   Local Transport:  The communication channel used between the
      mobile agent and the constrained device during onboarding
      (e.g., BLE, NFC, proprietary short-range radio).

   Steady-State Transport:  The communication channel used between
      the constrained device and the authorization authority after
      onboarding (e.g., a low-power radio protocol, Wi-Fi, or
      cellular).  This may be the same as or different from the
      local transport.

   Enrollment:  The process by which a constrained device is
      registered with the authorization authority and provisioned
      with credentials for steady-state communication.

   Bootstrap:  The initial phase of enrollment during which no
      shared credentials exist between the device and the
      authority.

   Steady-State:  The operational phase after enrollment, during
      which the device and authority share a symmetric key and
      can communicate with mutual authentication.


3.  Scope and Non-Goals

3.1.  Scope

   This document addresses the design space of onboarding
   protocols for constrained devices that meet ALL of the
   following criteria:

   o  The device has no factory-provisioned identity (no IDevID,
      no manufacturer certificate, no pre-installed keys).

   o  The local transport provides confidentiality (encryption)
      but does not reliably authenticate the peer during initial
      contact.

   o  A human operator is involved in the onboarding decision,
      either directly (pressing a button, scanning a code) or
      through a delegated mobile agent.

   o  The device is too constrained -- in power, memory, or
      connectivity -- to perform multi-round key exchange
      protocols or maintain persistent TLS/DTLS sessions.

   o  An authorization authority serves as the root of trust for
      the deployment and makes all final enrollment decisions.

   The guidance in this document is transport-agnostic.  While
   examples reference BLE and short-range radio protocols, the
   patterns apply to any local transport where encryption does
   not imply authentication.

3.2.  Non-Goals

   This document does NOT:

   o  Define a new protocol, message format, or wire encoding.

   o  Replace or compete with EDHOC [RFC9528], OSCORE [RFC8613],
      BRSKI [RFC8995], ACE-OAuth [RFC9200], or any other IETF
      standard.

   o  Provide a formal security proof or verification.  The
      patterns described are based on analysis of onboarding
      requirements and informal threat modeling.

   o  Address firmware update security (see SUIT [RFC9019]).

   o  Address post-onboarding application-layer encryption or
      access control.

3.3.  Relationship to Existing Standards

   Several IETF standards address aspects of constrained-device
   security.  This document addresses a gap that none of them
   individually fill:

   BRSKI [RFC8995]:  BRSKI provides automated, voucher-based
      onboarding but requires factory-provisioned X.509 identity
      certificates (IDevID) and a Manufacturer Authorized Signing
      Authority (MASA).  This document addresses systems where
      devices have no factory-provisioned identity and no
      manufacturer is in the trust chain.

   EDHOC [RFC9528]:  EDHOC provides a compact, formally analyzed
      authenticated key exchange.  However, it assumes the
      communicating parties already have a basis for mutual
      identification (e.g., pre-provisioned credentials or
      certificates).  This document addresses the prior step:
      how to establish that basis when the device is anonymous.

   ACE-OAuth [RFC9200]:  ACE provides authorization for
      constrained environments using OAuth 2.0 semantics.  It
      assumes the authorization server already knows which
      clients and resource servers exist.  This document
      addresses the bootstrap of that knowledge.

   OSCORE [RFC8613]:  OSCORE provides end-to-end object security
      for CoAP.  It assumes keys already exist (from EDHOC,
      DTLS, or out-of-band provisioning).  The patterns in this
      document are complementary: they describe how to establish
      keys that OSCORE (or similar protocols) can subsequently
      use.

   The patterns in this document compose well-understood
   cryptographic primitives and are designed to be compatible
   with adoption of the above standards when the system's
   constraints permit.


4.  Common Failure Modes

   This section describes design errors that commonly arise in
   constrained-device onboarding systems that operate over
   encrypted but unauthenticated local transports.

4.1.  Conflating Encryption with Authentication

   The most common failure mode is treating an encrypted channel
   as proof of peer identity.  When a BLE "Just Works" connection
   is established, the link is encrypted and integrity-protected,
   but the pairing process does not verify which device is at the
   other end.

   Systems that rely on the encrypted channel for identity make
   an implicit assumption: "because this channel is encrypted,
   the device I am communicating with is the one I intend."  This
   assumption is violated by any active attacker who can initiate
   or intercept a pairing session.

   The consequence is that credential material transmitted over
   the channel (such as a device key or enrollment payload) may
   be delivered to an attacker rather than the intended device.
   The system has no mechanism to detect this substitution.

   Mitigation: treat all local transport channels as untrusted
   for identity purposes.  Authenticate at the application layer,
   not the transport layer.

4.2.  Trusting the Agent to Validate

   A second failure mode occurs when the authorization authority
   delegates validation to the mobile agent.  If the authority
   accepts an enrollment request solely because the agent
   submitted it -- without independently verifying the agent's
   credential or the request's integrity -- then a compromised
   or rogue agent can enroll arbitrary devices.

   Mitigation: the authorization authority MUST independently
   validate every enrollment request, including verification of
   the agent's credential and any integrity checks on the
   enrollment payload.

4.3.  Permanent State from Unverified Bootstraps

   A third failure mode occurs when a device commits to permanent
   state changes (e.g., erasing a one-time enrollment payload)
   based on an unverified response.  If an attacker can forge an
   enrollment acknowledgement, the device may believe it is
   enrolled when the authority has no record of it.  If the device
   has already erased its enrollment payload, it cannot retry and
   is permanently stranded.

   Mitigation: defer irreversible state changes until the device
   has independently verified enrollment through normal
   authenticated communication with the authority (see
   Section 8.5).


5.  Threat Model Framework

   Onboarding protocols that operate over encrypted but
   unauthenticated transports face a specific set of threats that
   differ from those addressed by transport-layer security
   protocols.  This section establishes a framework for
   documenting trust boundaries, assumptions, and explicit
   limitations.

5.1.  Trust Boundaries

   Systems in this design space typically involve four classes of
   entity, each with distinct trust properties:

   Authorization Authority:  Root of trust for the deployment.
      Stores all device credentials, validates all enrollment
      requests, and enforces policy.  Assumed to operate in a
      controlled environment with a protected key store.

   Constrained Device:  Initially has no identity or credentials.
      After onboarding, authenticated via a unique per-device
      symmetric key.  Trusted for its own data once enrolled, but
      has no authority over other devices.

   Mobile Agent:  Holds a revocable credential issued by the
      authorization authority.  Trusted to initiate enrollment,
      but the authority independently validates every request the
      agent submits.

   Local Transport:  Provides confidentiality but NOT peer
      authentication during initial contact.  Treated as untrusted
      for identity purposes.

   A critical design principle is that the authorization authority
   MUST NOT delegate its validation responsibilities to the mobile
   agent.  The agent initiates enrollment; the authority decides
   whether to accept it.

5.2.  Core Assumptions

   The following assumptions underlie the design patterns in this
   document:

   o  The authorization authority is operated in a controlled
      environment with a protected key store.

   o  Device identity is bound to an embedded symmetric key.
      Physical access to the device is equivalent to key
      compromise.

   o  The authority has no persistent outbound connection to
      devices.  Communication is always device-initiated.

   o  The mobile agent's authority derives from a credential
      issued by the authorization authority.  The agent generates
      per-device keys and submits enrollment requests, but the
      authority independently validates every request.

   o  Compromise of a mobile agent's credential enables
      unauthorized enrollment of new devices, but does not
      affect devices already enrolled (each has an independent
      key).

5.3.  Documenting Explicit Non-Goals

   Systems operating in this design space SHOULD explicitly
   document which threats they do NOT address.  Common examples
   include:

   Confidentiality of Device Data:  Many constrained-device
      protocols authenticate messages (via MAC or signature) but
      do not encrypt payloads.  If the application requires
      confidentiality, it MUST be provided at a higher layer.

   Authorization Authority Compromise:  If the authority's key
      store is compromised, all device keys are exposed.  This
      is an operational security problem, not a protocol problem.

   Physical-Layer Denial of Service:  Radio jamming, BLE
      interference, and similar physical-layer attacks are
      outside the scope of application-layer onboarding
      protocols.

   The value of explicitly documenting non-goals is that
   reviewers and auditors can evaluate the system against its
   stated threat model rather than against an implicit (and often
   unrealistic) assumption of universal security.


6.  Bootstrap Versus Steady-State Security

   A common source of confusion in constrained-device protocols
   is the failure to distinguish between bootstrap security (the
   properties available during initial onboarding) and steady-
   state security (the properties available after credentials
   have been established).

6.1.  The Bootstrap Problem

   During bootstrap, the constrained device has no pre-existing
   credentials.  The onboarding protocol must establish a shared
   secret between the device and the authorization authority,
   using only a local transport that does not authenticate peers.

   This creates a fundamental asymmetry: the authorization
   authority can verify the mobile agent's identity (via the
   agent's credential), but neither the authority nor the agent
   can cryptographically verify the identity of the constrained
   device during first contact.  The device is, by definition,
   anonymous until onboarding completes.

   Protocols that attempt to solve this problem by layering
   additional key exchanges on top of the unauthenticated
   transport are solving a different problem.  The core issue is
   not the absence of a key exchange mechanism; it is the absence
   of any prior basis for knowing WHICH device is being
   communicated with.

   Effective bootstrap designs therefore focus on:

   o  Concentrating trust decisions in the authorization
      authority.

   o  Ensuring that the consequences of a compromised bootstrap
      are bounded and recoverable.

   o  Making the human operator's intent (pairing THIS device)
      as explicit as the transport allows.

6.2.  Dual-Transport Architectures

   In many constrained-device systems, the local transport used
   for onboarding (e.g., BLE) differs from the steady-state
   transport used for ongoing communication (e.g., a low-power
   radio protocol).  This dual-transport architecture arises
   naturally when:

   o  The constrained device lacks the resources to maintain a
      persistent BLE connection but can transmit short radio
      frames.

   o  The authorization authority is reachable over the steady-
      state transport but not over the local transport.

   o  The mobile agent bridges the two transports during
      onboarding but is not involved in steady-state operation.

   In such architectures, the constrained device relays an
   opaque enrollment payload from the local transport to the
   authorization authority over the steady-state transport.  The
   device does not interpret the payload and cannot forge one.

   The design patterns in Sections 7 and 8 accommodate both
   single-transport and dual-transport architectures.

6.3.  Session Lifecycle: A Reference Pattern

   This section describes one pattern for session management that
   is well-suited to sleep-based constrained devices.  Other
   patterns exist (e.g., persistent counters with flash wear-
   leveling, epoch-based schemes, or challenge-response per
   message).  The pattern described here optimizes for zero
   persistent state on the device.

   1. The device wakes from deep sleep and transmits a session-
      initiation message containing a fresh random nonce
      generated by a hardware random number generator.

   2. The authorization authority responds with an authenticated
      message that includes a random starting sequence number,
      binding the response to the device's nonce.

   3. All subsequent messages in the session use incrementing
      sequence numbers derived from the authority-assigned
      starting value.

   4. The device completes its work and enters deep sleep.
      Neither the device nor the authority persists any replay-
      protection state across sleep cycles.

   This pattern has two important properties: it requires no
   persistent sequence counters (avoiding flash-wear on
   constrained devices), and it binds each session to a fresh
   random nonce (preventing cross-session replay).

   Alternative patterns include:

   o  Persistent monotonic counters stored in flash with wear-
      leveling.  Suitable for devices with sufficient flash
      write budget.

   o  Epoch-based schemes where the authority maintains a
      coarse-grained epoch counter and rejects messages from
      previous epochs.

   o  Per-message challenge-response, where each message
      includes a fresh challenge.  Suitable for low-frequency
      communication but doubles message count.

6.4.  Replay Protection Without Persistent State

   Traditional replay protection requires at least one party to
   maintain a monotonically increasing counter across sessions.
   This is impractical for devices that deep-sleep between every
   communication cycle and have limited flash-write budgets.

   The reference pattern described in Section 6.3 achieves
   replay protection through a combination of:

   o  A fresh random nonce per session (generated by the
      device).

   o  A random starting sequence number per session (assigned
      by the authority in an authenticated response).

   o  Strictly incrementing sequence numbers within a session.

   An attacker who replays a captured session-initiation message
   causes the authority to create a new session with a DIFFERENT
   random starting sequence.  The attacker cannot generate valid
   follow-up messages because they lack the device's symmetric
   key to compute the message authentication code.  Replayed
   post-initiation messages from a previous session carry stale
   sequence numbers and are rejected.

6.5.  Why Session-Initiation Replay Is Tolerable

   Session-initiation messages can themselves be replayed by an
   attacker.  This is tolerable because:

   o  The attacker cannot forge authenticated follow-up messages
      without the device's symmetric key.

   o  The authority creates a new session that simply times out
      with no further activity.  No state is corrupted.

   o  The real device's next wake cycle creates an independent
      session unaffected by the phantom.

   The operational cost is limited to a spurious log entry and
   possibly a wasted command to a device that is not listening.
   Both are self-correcting.


7.  Delegated Onboarding Authorities

7.1.  The Mobile Agent Pattern

   In many deployments, the authorization authority is not
   directly accessible to the constrained device during
   onboarding.  A mobile agent bridges this gap.

   The mobile agent pattern involves three phases:

   Phase 1 - Agent Registration:  The mobile agent registers
      with the authorization authority and receives a unique
      agent credential (e.g., a 256-bit pre-shared key).  This
      is a one-time operation per agent.

   Phase 2 - Device Provisioning:  The mobile agent generates a
      unique per-device credential, constructs an enrollment
      request authenticated with its agent credential, encrypts
      the request with the authority's public key, and transmits
      both the device credential and the encrypted enrollment
      request to the constrained device over the local transport.

   Phase 3 - Authority Validation:  The constrained device
      relays the encrypted enrollment request to the
      authorization authority over the steady-state transport
      (see Section 6.2).  The authority decrypts the request,
      verifies the agent's authentication, extracts the device
      credential, and registers the device.

   The following diagram illustrates the message flow:

     Mobile        Constrained    Authorization
     Agent           Device        Authority
       |                |              |
       |  Phase 1: Agent Registration  |
       |------------------------------>|
       |    agent credential issued    |
       |<------------------------------|
       |                |              |
       |  Phase 2: Device Provisioning |
       |  device_cred + encrypted_req  |
       |--------------->|              |
       |                |              |
       |  Phase 3: Authority Validation|
       |                | relay(enc_req)|
       |                |------------->|
       |                |  enroll_ack  |
       |                |<-------------|
       |                |              |

   The constrained device acts as a relay for an opaque,
   encrypted payload.  It does not interpret the enrollment
   request and cannot forge one.

7.2.  Agent Credential Lifecycle

   Agent credentials SHOULD support the following operations:

   Issuance:  The authority generates a unique credential and
      transmits it to the agent over a secured channel.

   Usage:  The agent authenticates every enrollment request
      with its credential.  The authority verifies the
      credential before accepting any request.

   Revocation:  The authority operator can revoke an agent
      credential at any time.  Revoked credentials are retained
      for audit purposes, but all future requests authenticated
      with the revoked credential are rejected.

7.3.  Trust Properties of Delegation

   A well-designed delegation model provides the following
   properties:

   Authorization:  Only agents holding a valid (non-revoked)
      credential can enroll new devices.

   Isolation:  Each agent has a unique credential.  One agent
      cannot forge requests as another.

   Auditability:  The authority records which agent credential
      was used to enroll each device.

   Revocability:  Revoking an agent credential immediately
      disables that agent's enrollment authority.

   No Authority Key Exposure:  The agent credential is a
      symmetric pairing credential.  It does not grant access
      to the authority's private key, device key database, or
      other authority state.

7.4.  Registration Windows

   To prevent unauthorized agents from silently enrolling by
   connecting to the authority's local interface, the authority
   SHOULD implement a registration window:

   o  Agent registration requests are rejected unless a
      registration window is currently open.

   o  The window is opened only by explicit operator action
      (e.g., a physical button press or an authenticated
      administrative command).

   o  The window closes automatically after a configurable
      duration (e.g., 120 seconds).

   This ensures that agent registration requires both possession
   of a valid local transport connection AND contemporaneous
   operator intent.

7.5.  Trust-On-First-Use for Authority Identity

   When a mobile agent connects to an authorization authority
   for the first time, there is typically no pre-existing trust
   anchor.  An attacker could present their own keypair and
   produce a valid response.

   The recommended approach is Trust-On-First-Use (TOFU) with
   key pinning:

   1. The agent establishes that the authority holds a specific
      public key (e.g., via a challenge-response signature, a
      displayed fingerprint, or a QR code).

   2. The agent persists the authority's public key and a stable
      authority identifier (pin-on-first-use).

   3. Subsequent connections reject any authority presenting a
      different public key.

   Specific mechanisms for establishing the initial binding
   include:

   o  Challenge-response: the agent sends a random challenge;
      the authority signs the challenge and its identifier with
      its private key; the agent verifies the signature.

   o  Manual verification: the operator compares a fingerprint
      of the authority's public key displayed on both devices.

   o  Out-of-band channel: the authority's public key is
      transferred via QR code, NFC tap, or USB.

   For environments requiring stronger assurance than TOFU, the
   operator SHOULD verify the authority's public key fingerprint
   out-of-band before relying on the pinned key.

   When using challenge-response, including the authority
   identifier in the signed payload binds the signature to a
   specific authority instance, preventing replay of a signature
   obtained from a different authority.


8.  Handling Unavoidable Races

   When the local transport does not authenticate peers, certain
   race conditions are inherent and cannot be eliminated by
   protocol design alone.  The goal is to detect, bound, and
   recover from these races rather than pretend they do not
   exist.

8.1.  The Transport-Layer MITM Race

   If the local transport uses an unauthenticated pairing model
   (e.g., BLE "Just Works"), an active man-in-the-middle
   attacker can intercept the device credential during
   provisioning.  The attacker can then race the legitimate
   device to submit an enrollment request to the authorization
   authority.

   This race is INHERENT to the transport model and cannot be
   eliminated without upgrading to an authenticated pairing
   method (e.g., BLE Passkey Entry or Numeric Comparison).

   Systems that accept this risk SHOULD implement the
   mitigations described in Sections 8.2 through 8.5 to bound
   the impact.

8.2.  One-Time-Use Registration Payloads

   The authorization authority SHOULD enforce that each device
   identifier can be registered at most once.  If an attacker
   wins the race and registers first using the intercepted
   credential, the duplicate registration attempt from the
   legitimate device is rejected.

   This does not prevent the attack, but it ensures that:

   o  The attacker's registration "burns" the device
      identifier.  The attacker cannot register additional
      devices with the same payload.

   o  The operator can detect the conflict (two parties
      claiming the same identifier) and investigate.

   o  The legitimate device can be factory-reset and
      re-provisioned with a new identifier and credential.

8.3.  Timestamp-Bounded Replay Windows

   Enrollment requests SHOULD include a timestamp set by the
   mobile agent.  The authorization authority SHOULD reject
   requests whose timestamps fall outside a tolerance window
   (e.g., +/-24 hours).

   This limits the window during which a captured enrollment
   request can be replayed, but does not prevent same-day
   attacks.  It is a defense-in-depth measure, not a primary
   mitigation.

8.4.  Binding Acknowledgements to Requests

   When the authorization authority acknowledges a successful
   enrollment, the acknowledgement SHOULD be cryptographically
   bound to the specific enrollment request.  An example
   pattern is:

      registration_proof = MAC(device_key,
         context_string || enrollment_payload)

   where:

   o  device_key is the newly provisioned per-device key.

   o  context_string is a fixed, protocol-specific string that
      prevents cross-protocol confusion.

   o  enrollment_payload is the exact bytes of the encrypted
      enrollment request that was relayed by the device.

   The device verifies the registration_proof before accepting
   the acknowledgement.  This prevents an attacker who
   intercepted the device credential from forging a substitute
   acknowledgement for a different enrollment.

   Other binding mechanisms (e.g., a signature over a hash of
   the enrollment payload) are equally valid.  The essential
   property is that the acknowledgement is tied to the specific
   enrollment request the device relayed.

8.5.  Self-Healing from Forged Acknowledgements

   Even with bound acknowledgements (Section 8.4), an attacker
   who intercepted the device credential over the local
   transport has all the material needed to compute a valid
   registration_proof.

   To handle this case, the device SHOULD implement deferred
   payload erasure:

   1. After accepting a registration_proof, the device sets a
      "registration complete" flag but does NOT immediately
      erase the enrollment payload.

   2. On its next wake cycle, the device attempts normal
      authenticated communication with the authorization
      authority.

   3. If the authority responds with a valid authenticated
      message (proving it knows the device's key and has the
      device in its registry), the device erases the
      enrollment payload permanently.

   4. If the authority does not respond or responds with an
      authentication failure, the device clears the
      "registration complete" flag and reverts to enrollment
      mode on its next wake cycle.

   This self-healing behavior ensures that a forged
   acknowledgement does not permanently strand the device in
   a state where it believes it is enrolled but the authority
   has no record of it.


9.  Identity Destruction and Re-Creation

   In many constrained-device systems, the appropriate response
   to key compromise is not key rotation but identity
   destruction.  This is a deliberate design choice, not a
   limitation.

   When a device's symmetric key is compromised:

   1. The authorization authority removes the compromised
      device from its registry.

   2. The device is factory-reset, which erases: the
      symmetric key (identity), any persistent application
      state, and any resident program or configuration.

   3. After reset, the device is inert -- it cannot
      authenticate with any authority and will not execute
      application logic.

   4. The operator re-provisions the device via the normal
      onboarding flow, establishing a new key and a new
      identity.

   This destroy-and-recreate model has several advantages over
   in-place key rotation:

   o  Simplicity: no key-rotation protocol is needed on the
      constrained device.

   o  Clean state: any application state that may have been
      influenced by the attacker is also erased.

   o  Auditability: the new identity is a distinct entry in
      the authority's registry, making it clear that a
      re-provisioning event occurred.

   The cost is operational: an operator must physically
   interact with the device (or be in BLE range) to re-
   provision it.  For systems where this is acceptable, it is
   a simpler and more robust approach than managing key
   rotation on a constrained device.


10. Security Considerations

   This entire document is about security considerations for
   constrained-device onboarding.  This section highlights
   three cross-cutting concerns.

10.1.  Silent Discard

   Protocols operating over unauthenticated transports SHOULD
   handle all authentication and integrity failures by silent
   discard.  No error response should be sent to the sender.

   Silent discard prevents information leakage that could
   assist an attacker:

   o  No indication of whether a key-lookup hint matched any
      known device.

   o  No indication of whether a message authentication code
      was close to valid.

   o  No indication of whether the authority has an active
      session for the claimed device.

   o  No indication of whether the message was syntactically
      well-formed.

   Constrained devices operating in sleep-based architectures
   already expect message loss due to radio unreliability.
   Silent discard is therefore compatible with existing retry
   logic (await response or timeout, then retry or sleep).

10.2.  Key Compromise Containment

   The per-device, per-agent keying model described in this
   document provides natural compromise containment:

   Device Key Compromise:  Limited to the compromised device.
      Other devices are unaffected.  The authority removes the
      compromised device from its registry.  The device can be
      factory-reset and re-provisioned with a new key (see
      Section 9).

   Agent Credential Compromise:  Enables the attacker to forge
      enrollment requests for new devices, but does NOT affect
      devices already enrolled (each has an independent key).
      The operator revokes the compromised credential; all
      future requests authenticated with it are rejected.
      Devices enrolled before revocation remain valid unless
      individually removed.

   These properties depend on each device and each agent
   having a unique, independent key.  Shared or derived keys
   weaken containment.

10.3.  Residual Risks

   The patterns in this document reduce but do not eliminate
   risk.  Designers SHOULD acknowledge the following residual
   risks in their threat models:

   o  TOFU (Section 7.5) is vulnerable to an active attacker
      during first contact between the mobile agent and the
      authorization authority.  Out-of-band verification of
      the authority's public key fingerprint mitigates this
      risk.

   o  BLE "Just Works" pairing (or equivalent unauthenticated
      transport pairing) permits active MITM interception of
      device credentials.  For high-threat environments, an
      authenticated pairing method (Passkey Entry, Numeric
      Comparison, or OOB) is required.

   o  The authorization authority is a single point of
      compromise.  If its key store is breached, all device
      keys are exposed.  Operational security measures
      (encrypted storage, access control, backup) are
      essential but outside the scope of protocol design.

   o  The patterns in this document compose well-understood
      cryptographic primitives (ECDH, AEAD, HMAC, CBOR) but
      have not been subjected to formal verification.
      Designers who require provable security guarantees
      should consider formally analyzed protocols such as
      EDHOC [RFC9528] for the key-exchange component, while
      recognizing that such protocols do not address the full
      onboarding problem described here.
